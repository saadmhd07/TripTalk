# TripTalk Product Strategy

## Product Thesis

TripTalk is not a generic language-learning app.

The product direction is:

- help people prepare for a country before arriving there
- reduce social stress before first real interactions
- offer culturally situated conversation practice without the pressure of talking to a real person

The value is not only "learn vocabulary".
The value is:

- feel more prepared
- understand local tone and expressions
- rehearse realistic situations
- discover cultural codes before being on the ground

## Initial ICP

Primary ICP:

- students preparing for an exchange semester or Erasmus
- young professionals preparing for a long trip, internship, PVT, or light expatriation
- people who over-prepare before travel
- people who want to practice without the pressure of real native speakers

Secondary ICP:

- language learners frustrated by overly academic tools
- curious travelers who want cultural immersion
- shy users who avoid human-to-human language exchange

Simple framing:

- people who want to feel socially and culturally ready before arriving in a country

## Positioning

Core promise:

- Prepare to experience a country before you arrive.

Expanded promise:

- TripTalk lets you practice realistic conversations with local characters and discover a country's social and cultural codes before being there.

Product pillars:

1. Situated conversation
2. Cultural immersion
3. Safe practice without judgment

TripTalk is not:

- a Duolingo clone
- a generic AI chatbot
- a Tandem replacement built around real humans
- an avatar-first gimmick

## Differentiation

Short term differentiation should come from:

- realistic travel and daily-life scenarios
- memorable local characters
- culturally specific language and tone
- useful cultural debrief after each session

Voice is a high-value next step because it directly improves immersion.

Avatar can matter later, but only after the conversation quality is strong enough to sustain the illusion.

## MVP Wedge

Recommended wedge:

- one country
- one strong cultural angle
- three to five excellent scenarios
- one or two memorable local characters

Recommended initial country:

- Chile

Reason:

- authentic founder story
- strong cultural identity
- more distinctive than generic US/UK English practice
- better narrative for demo and distribution

Recommended first scenarios:

1. Airport arrival
2. Taxi / rideshare
3. Cafe or casual food stop
4. Free conversation with a local
5. Housing / flatshare / first-days logistics

## Validation Strategy

At this stage, the goal is not broad marketing.

The goal is controlled product learning.

Three phases:

1. Solidify the core
2. Run closed tests
3. Iterate based on repeated signals

Early testers can come from:

- personal travel network
- exchange-student friends
- friends-of-friends who traveled or are about to leave
- later: targeted Facebook groups and communities

## What Counts As Proof

Weak signals:

- "cool"
- "nice design"
- "fun"

Strong signals:

- "I would have used this before my trip"
- "This helps me project myself into the country"
- "This makes me feel less stressed"
- "I want to try more situations"
- "This feels more real than classic language apps"

The first useful proof target:

- 5 to 10 relevant testers
- each completes one full session
- at least some of them want a second session
- they can explain the value in their own words

## Readiness Before Testing

Before sharing widely, the product should be:

- stable enough to avoid obvious frustration
- clear enough that the promise is understood quickly
- polished enough that feedback is about the concept, not broken UX

"Ready to test" means:

- no blocking bugs in the main flow
- one country flow feels coherent
- conversations are credible
- cultural feedback adds value
- the UI feels intentional

It does not mean:

- perfect architecture
- many countries
- full avatar system
- complete growth infrastructure

## 10-Day Working Plan

### Day 1

- lock product framing in the repo
- align UI copy with the positioning
- define the exact "ready to test" bar

### Day 2

- audit current Chile and USA scenarios
- pick the primary wedge country
- identify weak/generic prompt and content areas

### Day 3

- improve one scenario end to end
- stronger intro
- stronger cultural hints
- better persona consistency

### Day 4

- improve the conversation screen experience
- make guidance, context, and feedback entry clearer

### Day 5

- improve feedback quality and structure
- make the debrief feel useful, not decorative

### Day 6

- add robustness: validation, errors, edge cases
- tighten the main flow

### Day 7

- add voice if scope allows
- otherwise prepare the architecture and interaction model for voice

### Day 8

- polish the visual identity of the wedge experience
- remove generic language-learning cues

### Day 9

- run 3 to 5 closed tests
- observe confusion, delight, and drop-off moments

### Day 10

- synthesize findings
- patch the most repeated issues
- prepare the next iteration or a public demo

## Working Rules

- optimize for clarity of product value
- avoid broadening scope too early
- prefer one excellent path over five average ones
- test earlier than feels comfortable, but not before the core experience is coherent
- build toward a product worth showing publicly, not just a technical prototype
